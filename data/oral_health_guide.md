# Oral Health Guidelines & Prevention

## Brushing and Flossing

### How to Brush Properly
- Use a soft-bristled toothbrush and fluoride toothpaste.
- Hold the brush at a 45-degree angle to your gums.
- Use short, gentle back-and-forth strokes.
- Brush all surfaces: outer, inner, and chewing surfaces of each tooth.
- Brush your tongue to remove bacteria and freshen breath.
- Brush for at least 2 minutes, twice a day (morning and before bed).
- Replace your toothbrush every 3-4 months or when bristles are frayed.

### How to Floss Properly
- Use about 18 inches of floss, winding most around your middle fingers.
- Hold the floss tightly between thumbs and forefingers.
- Gently guide the floss between teeth using a rubbing motion.
- Curve the floss into a C-shape against one tooth when it reaches the gum line.
- Slide it gently into the space between the gum and tooth.
- Floss at least once a day, preferably before bedtime.

### Mouthwash
- Mouthwash is a supplement, not a replacement for brushing and flossing.
- Antiseptic mouthwash helps reduce plaque and gingivitis.
- Fluoride mouthwash helps strengthen enamel.
- Do not eat or drink for 30 minutes after using mouthwash.

## Common Dental Problems

### Cavities (Tooth Decay)
Cavities are permanently damaged areas in the hard surface of teeth that develop into tiny holes. They are caused by bacteria, frequent snacking, sugary drinks, and poor oral hygiene. Prevention: brush twice daily, floss daily, limit sugary snacks, and visit the dentist regularly.

### Gingivitis (Early Gum Disease)
Gingivitis is inflammation of the gums caused by plaque buildup. Symptoms include red, swollen, and bleeding gums. It is reversible with good oral hygiene and professional cleaning. If left untreated, it can progress to periodontitis.

### Periodontitis (Advanced Gum Disease)
Periodontitis is a serious gum infection that damages soft tissue and can destroy the bone supporting your teeth. Symptoms include persistent bad breath, receding gums, loose teeth, and painful chewing. Treatment may include deep cleaning (scaling and root planing), medication, or surgery.

### Tooth Sensitivity
Sensitivity occurs when the protective enamel is worn down or gums recede, exposing the underlying dentin. Triggers include hot, cold, sweet, or acidic foods. Treatment options: desensitizing toothpaste, fluoride treatments, bonding, or gum grafts for severe cases.

### Bad Breath (Halitosis)
Common causes include poor oral hygiene, food particles, dry mouth, tobacco, and certain medical conditions. Solutions: brush and floss regularly, clean your tongue, stay hydrated, and visit the dentist to rule out underlying issues.

### Teeth Grinding (Bruxism)
Bruxism is the unconscious clenching or grinding of teeth, often during sleep. It can cause jaw pain, headaches, worn teeth, and cracked fillings. Treatment: custom night guard, stress management, and correcting misalignment.

## Dental Health for Children

### When to Start Dental Visits
- First dental visit should be by age 1 or when the first tooth appears.
- Children should visit the dentist every 6 months.
- Early visits help prevent cavities and establish good habits.

### Preventing Cavities in Children
- Do not put babies to bed with milk or juice bottles (causes bottle tooth decay).
- Start brushing with a rice-grain-sized amount of fluoride toothpaste at age 1-2.
- Use a pea-sized amount of fluoride toothpaste from age 3-6.
- Supervise brushing until age 7-8.
- Dental sealants can protect molars from cavities.

### Thumb Sucking and Pacifiers
- Most children stop on their own by age 2-4.
- Prolonged thumb sucking after age 4 can affect tooth alignment.
- If the habit persists past age 4, consult a dentist about intervention options.

## Dental Health During Pregnancy

- Hormonal changes increase the risk of gingivitis and tooth decay during pregnancy.
- Morning sickness acid can erode tooth enamel — rinse with water or baking soda solution after vomiting.
- Dental cleanings and necessary treatments are safe during pregnancy (second trimester is ideal).
- Inform your dentist that you are pregnant before any procedure.
- X-rays are generally avoided during pregnancy unless absolutely necessary.

## Nutrition and Dental Health

### Foods Good for Teeth
- Dairy products (cheese, yogurt, milk) — rich in calcium and phosphorus.
- Crunchy fruits and vegetables (apples, carrots, celery) — stimulate saliva and clean teeth.
- Leafy greens (spinach, kale) — high in calcium and folic acid.
- Green and black tea — contain polyphenols that suppress bacteria.
- Water — rinses away food particles and keeps mouth hydrated.

### Foods Bad for Teeth
- Sugary candies and sweets, especially sticky ones like caramel.
- Carbonated soft drinks — high in sugar and acid.
- Citrus fruits in excess — acid erodes enamel.
- Starchy foods that get stuck (chips, white bread).
- Ice — chewing ice can crack teeth and damage enamel.
- Coffee and red wine — stain teeth over time.

## Smoking and Oral Health

Tobacco use significantly impacts oral health:
- Increases risk of gum disease (3-6 times more likely).
- Delays healing after dental procedures.
- Causes tooth discoloration and bad breath.
- Greatly increases the risk of oral cancer.
- Reduces success rate of dental implants.
- Quitting tobacco improves oral health within weeks.

## Oral Cancer Awareness

### Risk Factors
- Tobacco use (cigarettes, cigars, chewing tobacco)
- Heavy alcohol consumption
- Excessive sun exposure (lip cancer)
- Human papillomavirus (HPV)
- Age over 40

### Warning Signs
- A sore in the mouth that does not heal within 2 weeks
- Red or white patches on gums, tongue, or mouth lining
- A lump or thickening in the cheek
- Difficulty chewing, swallowing, or moving the jaw
- Numbness in the tongue or mouth
- Persistent ear pain

### Early Detection
Regular dental check-ups include an oral cancer screening. Early detection greatly improves treatment outcomes. If you notice any warning signs, schedule an appointment immediately.
